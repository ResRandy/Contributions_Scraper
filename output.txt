Abstract
Gaining insight into the diversity, structure, and metabolic functions of microbial communities is essential for understanding their
roles in host health and ecosystem dynamics. However, research on the seahorse-associated microbiome remains limited, de-
spite these threatened ﬁsh facing increasing human pressures worldwide. Here, we explored the microbial diversity and meta-
bolic functions of the skin and gut of the tiger tail seahorse (Hippocampus comes) and its surrounding environment using
shotgun metagenomics and bioinformatics. Members of the Pseudomonadota phylum were dominant in the skin microbiome,
whereas Bacteroidota was dominant in the gut. Bacillota, Actinomycetota, and Planctomycetota were also detected in the sea-
horse-associated microbiome. Statistical analysis revealed signiﬁcant differences (P < 0.01) in species diversity between skin and
gut microbiomes, with members belonging to the Moraxellaceae family being dominant on the skin and the Bacteroidaceae
family in the gut. Moreover, the surrounding environment (water or sediment) did not have a direct effect on the seahorse micro-
biome composition. The skin microbiome exhibited a higher abundance of functional genes related to energy, lipid, and amino
acid metabolism as well as terpenoids and polyketides metabolism, xenobiotics biodegradation, and metabolism compared with
the gut. Despite differences among classes, the total abundance of bacteriocins was similar in both gut and skin microbiomes,
which is signiﬁcant in shaping microbial communities due to their antimicrobial properties. A better knowledge of seahorse
microbiomes beneﬁts conservation and sustainable aquaculture efforts, offering insights into habitat protection, disease manage-
ment, and optimizing aquaculture environments, thereby promoting seahorse health and welfare while minimizing environmental
impact and enhancing aquaculture sustainability.
NEW & NOTEWORTHY To the best of our knowledge, this study represents the ﬁrst comprehensive examination of the taxo-
nomic and functional patterns of the skin and gut microbiome in the tiger tail seahorse. These ﬁndings have the potential to sig-
niﬁcantly enhance our understanding of the seahorse-associated microbiome, thereby contributing to the prediction and control
of bacterial infections in seahorses, which are a leading cause of high mass mortality rates in seahorse aquaculture and other
ﬁsh species.

INTRODUCTION
Despite being the most emblematic ambassadors of marine
biodiversity, seahorses are globally exploited for traditional
medicine, the aquarium trade, and as ornamental curiosities,
resulting in a substantial decline in their wild populations (1–
3). Consequently, they are now listed as vulnerable species by
the International Union for Conservation of Nature Red List
of Threatened Species (4), with some species classiﬁed as
endangered or critically endangered. This critical situation
highlights the pressing need for the development of sustain-
able solutions to reduce exploitation and ensure the survival
of these species. Aquaculture represents one potential solu-
tion to counter the overexploitation of seahorses. However,

the delicate and specialized nature of seahorse diet and nutri-
tion as well as severe problems of mass mortality caused by
bacterial infections (5). Previous studies have shown that
aquatic species harbor a complex and diverse microbiome,
which plays a crucial role in their development and health (6,
7), with particular emphasis on the signiﬁcance of the intesti-
nal microbiota in regulating host metabolism, immunity, and
response to infections (8, 9). However, there is limited infor-
mation available on the microbial diversity and functional
patterns in seahorses, particularly for vulnerable species such
as the tiger tail seahorse (Hippocampus comes). To address
these gaps, our study investigated the microbial proﬁles of
seahorses’ skin and gut, as well as their surrounding envi-
ronments, using a combination of shotgun metagenomics
and bioinformatics. Our goal was to identify their associ-
ated microbiome and understand its roles, which could
be of great practical signiﬁcance for aquaculture initia-
tives, disease resistance, and overall seahorse health (7).
By uncovering the genetic functions of these bacteria, we
aimed to elucidate how the microbiome inﬂuences sea-
horse physiology and disease processes. This understand-
ing is crucial for developing targeted interventions to
mitigate bacterial infections and enhance seahorse aqua-
culture sustainability, thereby contributing to wild sea-
horse conservation efforts.
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Before conducting ﬁeldwork, all necessary permissions
were obtained from the local government unit of Surigao
City, and a gratuitous permit (GP No. 0184-19) was issued by
the Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources. Sampling
was conducted on Hanigad Island (9.8500 N, 125.5833 E) in
Surigao del Norte, Philippines, where areas were surveyed
randomly using scuba diving and snorkeling (10). Fifteen
healthy tiger tail seahorse (H. comes) specimens (n ¼ 15)
were collected in a single trip during January 2023, from 5
AM to 9 PM, using a nondestructive method that involved
the careful capture of seahorses with small handheld nets.
Water (3 L) and sediment (100 g) samples were also collected
using sterile containers at multiple predetermined locations
and depths, where seahorses were collected, to ensure uni-
form and representative sampling. Seahorses were subjected
to a 24-h fasting period to clear any residual dietary contents
for microbiome analysis. Seahorses were then euthanized,
and skin mucus was collected aseptically from their surfaces
using a sterile swab, starting from the head and moving
down to the tail. Subsequently, gut samples were collected
from the seahorses by making an incision on the lateral side
of the stomach using sterile dissecting scissors. The entire
gut was then rinsed with distilled water using a sterile sy-
ringe to obtain microbial biomass. Genomic DNA was
extracted from the gut and skin of tiger tail seahorses using
the DNeasy PowerSoil Pro Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) as
well as from the surrounding water and sediment using the
HiPurA Water and Soil DNA Puriﬁcation kits (HiMedia
Laboratories, Mumbai, India), respectively, following the
manufacturer’s
instructions
and
previously
described
methods (11). Equal amounts of DNA were pooled into
three groups, with each group containing DNA from ﬁve
specimens. Similarly, DNA samples extracted from water
or sediment were pooled into a single group, with each
group containing DNA from three samples. Subsequently,
these pooled samples were sequenced on the NovaSeq
6000 platform (Illumina, San Diego, CA) using 150-bp
paired-end reads. All quality checked reads were aligned
against the GenBank non-redundant (nr) protein database
(https://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/blast/db/FASTA/) using the
BLASTX command implemented in DIAMOND v2.1.7 (12).
For detailed taxonomic classiﬁcation, output ﬁles were ﬁl-
tered using the lowest common ancestor (LCA) algorithm
in MEGAN 6 (13), with parameters set at a min score of 50,
top percent of 10.0, and min support of 0.01. In addition,
the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG)
pathway database was used to ensure a comprehensive
and detailed functional annotation (14). Simultaneously,
the BAGEL4 database was used to detect bacteriocins (15).
For bacteriocin, the alignment was restricted to the best
match of the reads with a minimum identity of 90% over
90% sequence length. METAXA2 was used to determine
the number of reads assigned to 16S rRNA genes (16), and
these values were subsequently used to normalize the
abundance of bacteriocins.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Although the skin and gut microbiome are known to sig-
niﬁcantly inﬂuence metabolic, nutritional, physiological,
and immunological processes (17–19), our understanding
of the seahorse-associated microbiome, especially con-
cerning tiger tail seahorses, remains limited. Here, we
investigated the diversity and functionality of microbial
communities associated with the gut and skin of the tiger
tail seahorse, using a combination of shotgun metagenom-
ics and bioinformatics.
The analysis revealed that Pseudomonadota (formerly
Proteobacteria) were dominant across all samples except in
the gut samples. This supports previous ﬁndings in herbivo-
rous ﬁsh, where the gut microbiomes are typically character-
ized by a predominance of Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes,
speciﬁcally within the order Clostridia (20, 21). Overall,
Pseudomonadota accounted for the largest proportion of bac-
terial communities in all four environments, followed by
Bacillota and Bacteroidota. Moraxellaceae emerged as the pre-
dominant family in the skin samples (Fig. 1), particularly rep-
resented by members of the Acinetobacter and Psychrobacter
genera, a noteworthy ﬁnding as it represents the ﬁrst evidence
of this family in healthy seahorses.
The gut samples were dominated by Bacteroidaceae, pre-
dominantly represented by members of the Bacteroides and
Phocaeicola genera, which are commonly found in anaerobic
environments. Previous studies have demonstrated that
Bacteroidaceae are involved in several bacterial functional
gene categories within the active gut microbiome (22).
Although these data should be interpreted with caution due
to the low number of water or sediment samples analyzed,
the surrounding environment (water or sediment) did not
appear to have a direct effect on the seahorse microbiome
composition. Moreover, while the primary focus of this study
was the seahorse microbiome, the results can have signiﬁ-
cant implications for human health. The identiﬁcation of

wild and farmed ﬁsh species (24). Moreover, the high
number of members belonging to the Enterococcaceae

members from the Aeromonadaceae and Shewanellaceae
families, emphasizing signiﬁcant implications and the
need for monitoring and assessment. Among the major
implications for both human health and conservation
efforts, the presence of clinically relevant bacteria in ma-
rine ecosystems can serve as ideal indicators of broader
environmental pollution (26, 27), thereby informing the
development of public health policies and interventions aimed
at safeguarding human health. Incorporating these insights
into conservation planning and management practices enables
stakeholders to work toward the preservation of healthy ma-
rine ecosystems, thereby ensuring the well-being of both ma-
rine
organisms
and
human
populations.
Moreover,
the
presence of clinically relevant bacteria and pathogens within
aquaculture facilities poses signiﬁcant threats to the physiolog-
ical health of farmed seahorses, potentially leading to disease
outbreaks, elevated mortality rates, and compromised growth
and reproductive success (28). The Shannon diversity index
implemented in MEGAN 6 showed a signiﬁcantly higher diver-
sity (P < 0.01) in the skin samples (3.51±0.31) compared with
the gut samples (1.60±0.27), probably inﬂuenced by the
unique characteristics of the seahorse skin and its exposure
to diverse environmental microorganisms. These ﬁndings
highlight the complex interactions within the tiger tail sea-
horse microbiome and emphasize the role of environmental
factors in shaping microbial populations. Further studies are
therefore required to understand the underlying mechanisms
and their ecological implications for seahorses and their
ecosystems.
To elucidate such interactions, we conducted a compre-
hensive and detailed functional annotation based on the
KEGG pathway database. The skin exhibited a higher
abundance (P < 0.05) of functional genes related to energy
metabolism, lipid metabolism, amino acid metabolism,
terpenoids and polyketides metabolism, xenobiotics bio-
degradation, and metabolism compared with the gut (Fig.
2). This suggests that the skin and gut microbiome may be
involved in the breakdown, utilization, and detoxiﬁcation
of various compounds, including lipids, amino acids, and
xenobiotics (29).
Moreover, the skin microbiome may have the ability to de-
grade and metabolize foreign substances, such as pollutants
or toxins, through xenobiotic metabolism (30). Functional
genes related to xenobiotics biodegradation and metabolism
suggest the potential role of the skin and gut microbiome in
detoxiﬁcation processes, resilience to environmental stres-
sors, and adaptation to xenobiotic exposure (31, 32). The
genetic information processing category also showed a
higher abundance (P < 0.05) of functional genes related to
translation, replication, and repair in the skin compared
with the gut. Within the environmental information process-
ing category, the skin displayed a greater abundance of
genes related to membrane transport and signal transduc-
tion, suggesting its role in interactions with the external

We also identiﬁed bacteriocins using the BAGEL4 data-
base, whose results demonstrated that the relative abun-
dance of bacteriocins was higher in the skin compared with
the gut (Fig. 3), but the difference was not found to be statis-
tically signiﬁcant (P > 0.05).
Class III bacteriocins were relatively more abundant com-
pared with class I and class II in both samples. Speciﬁcally,
class I bacteriocins, known as lantibiotics, were more preva-
lent in the skin microbiome, and they have shown promising
antimicrobial properties and potential health beneﬁts as
probiotics (34). Both class II and class III bacteriocins have
also been of signiﬁcant interest as probiotics, as they can
enhance gastrointestinal health and combat pathogens in
the gut environment. Our analysis identiﬁed a total of 11 bac-
teriocins, with 5 of them being common to both skin and gut
samples. Notably, enterocins were prevalent in both samples
and have a wide range of antimicrobial activity against
gram-positive pathogens (35), making them valuable candi-
dates for further investigation. The presence and abundance
of bacteriocin classes in both skin and gut samples suggest a
potential capacity for antimicrobial activity in both micro-
biomes, offering insights into the dynamics of microbial
communities and their potential inﬂuence on the tiger tail
seahorse’s health.
Moreover, assessing the presence and abundance of these
bacteriocins in our study provides baseline information on
the role of the seahorse microbiome, which is essential for
their survival in contrasting environment. Previous studies
have suggested that the ﬁsh microbiome plays a crucial role
in how ﬁsh recognize and respond to pathogens through
their mucosal surfaces (8, 36). Microbiomes are also essential
for several physiological processes in ﬁsh, including nervous
system development, behavior, immunity, digestion, and
metabolism, all of which are critical for overall health (37).
Therefore, assessing the seahorse microbiome may reveal
important adaptations in these interactions, highlighting
their physiological signiﬁcance.

microbiome of the tiger tail seahorse, highlighting its ecolog-
ical and health importance. The skin and gut microbiomes
differ signiﬁcantly in composition and functional properties,
